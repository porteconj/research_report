Can a tax exemption for young, first-time buyers make housing more affordable, or will it simply be passed through in higher house prices? In 2024, the Portuguese government introduced an exemption from property transfer tax and stamp duty for individuals under 35 buying their first home. The policy was designed to reduce the upfront cost of buying a home and help young households enter the housing market. But in a market where supply is limited, lowering buyers' costs may also increase what sellers believe buyers can pay.

T﻿his paper studies sellers' reaction to the announcement of the policy by studying monthly house asking prices across Portuguese municipalities and comparing them with a carefully constructed counterfactual based on Spanish municipalities. This comparison allows the study of price changes with and without the policy.

The results suggest that sellers passed through some of the tax exemption. After the announcement, the monthly growth rate of real asking prices in Portugal increased by about 0.26 percentage points relative to the counterfactual (an equivalent cumulative annual effect of 3.2%). The effect was stronger in the metropolitan areas of Lisbon and Porto, in lower-income municipalities, and in places with a larger share of young potential buyers. Depending on the price range, the estimated tax savings could be offset within a few months to just over a year. 

The implication is not that eligible buyers receive no benefit at all, but that part of the intended tax relief may be quickly absorbed by higher asking prices. The broader lesson is that demand-side housing policies, when introduced without measures to expand supply, may have unintended consequences for affordability.
