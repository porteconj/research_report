How do individuals react when governments tax unexpected “windfall” gains? While most models of taxation focus on predictable income, windfall income is fundamentally different since by its very definition it is unpredictable. Governments often justify taxes on windfall gains, such as speculative investments or lottery prizes, on equity and fiscal grounds. Yet, because these gains are unpredictable, individuals cannot easily adjust their behavior in advance in the same way they can with labor income or savings decisions. This raises an important question: do consumers respond to taxes on unexpected gains at all?

T﻿his paper studies the introduction of lottery taxes in Spain and Portugal in 2013. Spain imposed a 20% tax on lottery prizes above €2,500, while Portugal introduced a similar 20% tax on prizes exceeding €5,000. Both countries participate in EuroMillions, Europe’s largest lottery game, where jackpots and winning probabilities are identical across participating countries. This setting provides a quasi-natural experiment to examine whether taxing lottery prizes affects lottery participation.

The analysis uses draw-by-draw EuroMillions sales data between 2010 and 2013 and applies the Synthetic Difference-in-Differences methodology to estimate how ticket sales evolved after the tax reforms relative to a counterfactual scenario without the policy changes.

The results suggest heterogeneous effects from lottery taxation. Aggregate EuroMillions sales remained broadly unchanged after the reforms. However, ticket sales declined significantly when jackpots were relatively small, while no meaningful effects were observed for very large jackpots. In other words, consumers appeared sensitive to taxation only when the potential prize was not extraordinarily high. When jackpots reach very high levels, the attractiveness of a life-changing prize appears to outweigh the effect of taxation.
